General and Middle Term of Binomial Expansion

In the expansion of \((x+a)^n\), the \((r+1)^{th}\) (convince yourself) term is \(\binom{n}{r}x^{n-r}a^r\). So we can also write \[(x+a)^n=\sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r}x^{n-r}a^r.\]

Sometimes, we are interested to know the middle term of the binomial expansion. Remember that there are \((n+1)\) terms in the expansion of \((a+b)^n=\sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r}b^r\).

  • If \(n\) is even, The number of terms, \((n+1)\) is odd and thus the middle term occurs at \(\left(\dfrac{(n+1)+1}{2}\right)^{th}\) or at the \(\left(\dfrac{n}{2}+1\right)^{th}\) term

  • If \(n\) is odd, The number of terms, \((n+1)\) is even and thus there are 2 middle terms occurring at \(\left(\dfrac{n+1}{2}\right)^{th}\) and \(\left(\dfrac{n+1}{2} + 1\right)^{th}\) or at the \(\left(\dfrac{n+1}{2}\right)^{th}\) and \(\left(\dfrac{n+3}{2}\right)^{th}\) terms.


If the \(4^{th}\) term in the expansion of \(\left(px+\frac{1}{x}\right)^n\) is independent of \(x\), find the value of \(n\). Also calculate \(p\) if the \(4^{th}\) term is \(5/2\).


The \(4^{th}\) term is \(\binom{n}{3}(px)^3\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^{n-3}=\binom{n}{3}p^3x^{6-n}\). If it is independent of \(x\), its power should be zero. So \(n=6\). Also since this \(4^{th}\) term is equal to \(5/2\), we have \(\binom{6}{3}\cdot p^3=\dfrac{5}{2}\implies p=\left(\dfrac{5}{2\cdot\binom{6}{3}}\right)^{1/3}=\dfrac{1}{2}\)


Find the middle term in the expansion of \((1-2x+x^2)^n\).


We have \((1-2x+x^2)^n=((1-x)^2)^n=(1-x)^{2n}\). \(2n\) is an even integer \(\implies (\frac{2n}{2}+1)^{th}\) i.e \((n+1)^{th}\) term will be the middle term. Now the \((n+1)^{th}\) term in \((1-x)^{2n}\) = \(^{2n}C_{n}(1)^{2n-n}(-x)^n\) = \(\dfrac{(2n)!}{n!\cdot n!} (-x)^n\)
