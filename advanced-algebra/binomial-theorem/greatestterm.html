Greatest Term in Binomial Expansion

Sometimes, it is interesting to find the greatest term in a binomial expansion. Let us find the greatest coefficient in the binomial expansion, \((1+x)^n\), where \(n\) is a positive integer. Now, \[\dfrac{\text{Coefficient of }(r+1)^{th}\text{ term}}{\text{Coefficient of }r^{th}\text{ term}}=\dfrac{\binom{n}{r}}{\binom{n}{r-1}}=\dfrac{\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}}{\frac{n!}{(r-1)!(n-r+1)!}}=\dfrac{n-r+1}{r}\] So the coefficient of the \((r+1)^{th}\) term will be greater or equal to than that of the \(r^{th}\) term as long as \(\dfrac{n-r+1}{r}\geq1\implies n-r+1\geq r \implies r\leq \dfrac{n+1}{2}\). But \(r\) must be an integer, and therefore
When \(n\) is even, the greatest binomial coefficient is given by the greatest value of \(r\) i.e., \(r=n/2\) and hence the greatest binomial coefficient is \(\binom{n}{n/2}\) (Middle Term)
When \(n\) is odd, \(n+1\) is divisible by 2. Thus, \(r=(n+1)/2\) will give the greatest binomial coefficient \(\binom{n}{(n+1)/2}\). Also, note that \(\binom{n}{(n-1)2}=\binom{n}{n-(n+1)/2}\), both values of \(r\), \((n-1)/2\) and \((n+1)/2\) (Middle Terms) give us the greatest binomial coefficient.


The greatest binomial coefficient in the expansion of \((1+x)^n\) (where \(n\in\mathbb{N}\)) occurs for the middle term.

Extending \(\mid T_{r+1} \mid\geq\mid T_r \mid\)

Let us now extend our proposition to more general expansion \((a+x)^n\). To determine the numerically greatest term in expansion of \((a+x)^n\), where \(n\) is a positive integer, consider \(\mid{\dfrac{T_{r+1}}{T_r}}\mid=\mid{\dfrac{\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}x^r}{\binom{n}{r-1}a^{n-r+1}x^{r-1}}}\mid=\mid{\dfrac{\binom{n}{r}}{\binom{n}{r-1}}}\mid\mid{\dfrac{x}{a}}\mid=\mid{\dfrac{n-r+1}{r}}\mid\mid{\dfrac{x}{a}}\mid\). Thus \(\mid{T_{r+1}}\mid\geq\mid{T_r}\mid\implies\{\dfrac{n+1}{r}-1\}\mid{\dfrac{x}{a}}\mid\geq1\) \[\mid{T_{r+1}}\mid\geq\mid{T_r}\mid\implies r\leq\dfrac{n+1}{1+\mid{\dfrac{a}{x}}\mid}\]

Do not memorize this proposition. Just understand the fact that in order to find the greatest term, use \(\mid{\dfrac{T_{r+1}}{T_r}}\mid\geq1\).

Given that the \(4^{th}\) term in the expansion of \(\left(2+\dfrac{3}{8}x\right)^{10}\) has the maximum numerical value, find the range of values of \(x\) for which this will be true.


Given \(4^{th}\) term in \(\left(2+\dfrac{3}{8}x\right)^{10}=2^{10}\left(1+\dfrac{3}{16}x\right)^{10}\), is numerically greatest \(\mid{\dfrac{T_4}{T_3}}\mid\geq1\) and \(\mid{\dfrac{T_5}{T_4}}\mid\leq1\implies\)\(\mid{\dfrac{\binom{10}{3}}{\binom{10}{2}}\dfrac{3}{16}x}\mid\geq1\) and \(\mid{\dfrac{\binom{10}{4}}{\binom{10}{3}}\dfrac{3}{16}x}\mid\leq1\implies\)\(\mid{x}\mid\geq2\) and \(\mid{x}\mid\leq\dfrac{64}{21}\implies\) \(x\in\left[-\dfrac{64}{21},-2\right]\bigcup\left[2,\dfrac{64}{21}\right]\)
